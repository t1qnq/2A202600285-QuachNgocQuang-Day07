# Cancer Screening

## Overview

This evidence-based companion guide on cancer screening explores the steps for improving colorectal, breast, and cervical cancer screening in health centers. Used alongside the Evidence-Based Care Action Guide, it offers health centers an actionable roadmap to cancer screening within the context of whole person care.

## Why Is Cancer Screening Important?

The Value Transformation Framework (VTF) is designed to guide health center cancer screening efforts using a systems approach to change. The VTF serves as an organizing framework with action steps that drive improved health outcomes, improved patient and staff experiences, reduced costs, and equity.

Cancer is the second leading cause of death in the United States. Early screening saves lives. Health centers play an important role in cancer screening and early detection. This guide provides evidence-based action steps for the screening of three cancers: colorectal, cervical, and breast.

The burden of cancer is profound. Hundreds of thousands of adults are affected each year, and cancer screening rates remain below target for all three cancers. Community health centers play a critical role in providing care and preventive services to vulnerable populations, and improving screening rates can help achieve Healthy People 2030 goals and the Quintuple Aim.

## What Are the Clinical Guidelines for Cancer Screening?

### Colorectal Cancer

Screening with colonoscopy every 10 years or annual high-sensitivity stool-based tests such as gFOBT or FIT can decrease colorectal cancer incidence and mortality. Positive stool tests require follow-up colonoscopy.

Clinical guidelines recommend screening average-risk adults aged 45 to 75 for colorectal cancer. Adults 76 to 85 years of age may be screened depending on overall health and personal preferences. Screening should be considered earlier for individuals at elevated risk, such as those with family history, inflammatory bowel disease, or hereditary syndromes.

### Cervical Cancer

The USPSTF recommends cervical cytology alone every 3 years for women aged 21 to 29. For women aged 30 to 65, screening options include cytology alone every 3 years, hrHPV testing alone every 5 years, or co-testing every 5 years. Screening is not recommended for women who have had a hysterectomy with cervix removed and no relevant history, women younger than 21, or women over 65 with adequate prior screening and no other risk.

The American Cancer Society revised its guidelines in 2021, increasing the age to start cervical screening to 25 years. Two main screening tests are Pap testing and HPV testing. The CDC recommends HPV vaccination for children around ages 11 or 12.

### Breast Cancer

USPSTF guidance has recommended biennial mammography for women aged 50 to 74, and ACS guidance allows annual screening beginning at age 40 with annual or biennial screening from age 55 onward. High-risk women may need mammography and MRI annually starting around age 30.

## How Can Health Centers Improve Cancer Screening?

The guide presents a 10-step Value Transformation Framework for improving cancer screening in health centers.

### Step 1: Design Models of Care

Design workflows and interventions that support evidence-based cancer screening services. Define when and how patients are screened, what testing options are available, how referrals and follow-up are handled, and how results are documented.

### Step 2: Create or Update Clinical Policies and Standing Orders

Create cancer screening clinical policies, procedures, and standing orders based on current evidence-based best practices. For colorectal cancer screening, use high-sensitivity stool-based tests. Colonoscopies are needed for high-risk patients and for all patients with positive screening results.

### Step 3: Engage Leadership

Set cancer screening as a top organizational priority. Leadership should set short- and long-term targets, provide performance feedback, and incorporate screening into the broader business case for value transformation.

### Step 4: Apply Population Health Management Strategies

Use risk stratification and registries to identify patients in need of screening or follow-up. Benchmark performance against local, state, and national standards.

### Step 5: Deploy Care Teams in New Ways

Train staff on the latest screening tests and strategies. Build proficiency checklists, pre-visit planning, huddles, and workflows that leverage the full care team.

### Step 6: Optimize Health Information Systems

Use structured EHR fields, gap reports, reminders, and tracking logs to document screenings, close care gaps, and recall patients who have not completed screening.

### Step 7: Engage Patients

Educate patients with multilingual materials, visuals, teach-back, reminders, and navigation support. Patient reminders and mailed stool-based tests can improve completion rates.

### Step 8: Develop or Enhance Community Partnerships

Build partnerships with colonoscopists, endoscopists, and other community partners. Formalize expectations with MOUs and monitor quality of colonoscopy reporting and follow-up.

### Step 9: Tailor Treatment for Social Context

Assess social risk factors such as transportation, food insecurity, housing instability, and financial barriers. Refer patients to community resources and document social risk in structured EHR fields.

### Step 10: Maximize Reimbursement

Collect reimbursement for all eligible care and services. Explore care management and other service lines that support cancer screening and generate additional revenue.
