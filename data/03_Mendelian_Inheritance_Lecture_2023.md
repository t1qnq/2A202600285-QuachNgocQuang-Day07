## Introduction

The founder of the modern science of genetics was Johann Gregor Mendel. Mendel's pea plant experiments established many of the rules of heredity, now referred to as the laws of Mendelian inheritance.

### Mendel's laws

**Law of Segregation**  
The two alleles for a heritable character segregate during gamete formation and end up in different gametes.

**Law of Independent Assortment**  
Separate genes for separate traits are passed independently of one another from parents to offspring.

**Law of Dominance**  
Recessive alleles are masked by dominant alleles. A cross between a homozygous dominant and a homozygous recessive will express the dominant phenotype while still having a heterozygous genotype.

**A Mendelian trait** is controlled by a single locus in an inheritance pattern. In such cases, a mutation in a single gene can cause a disease that is inherited according to Mendel's laws. These diseases are called monogenic diseases.

Mendelian inheritance is the basis of classical genetics. Even though knowledge about classical genetics has expanded, the heredity of human diseases and traits can still be related to Mendelian inheritance.

The classical classification of hereditary patterns includes:

- Autosomal dominant
- Autosomal recessive
- Codominant
- X-linked dominant
- X-linked recessive
- Y-linked

The genetics of human traits is difficult to study because it is not possible to make backcrosses, the time lag between generations is much longer than in classical model organisms, and family sizes are significantly smaller.

## Interpretation of Some Basic Genetic Terms

**Gene:** A DNA sequence coding for one or more functional products.

**Allele:** An alternative form of a gene located at a specific locus on a specific chromosome. A diploid cell contains two alleles at a time.

**Allelic heterogeneity:** Different mutant alleles of the same gene result in similar symptoms or diseases.

**Dominance / recessivity:** These terms refer to phenotype, not genotype. A trait is dominant when it is visible even in heterozygous form.

**Modifier genes:** Genes that influence the expression of another gene. These are interactions between two or more genes of different loci.

**Co-dominance:** Both alleles are manifested phenotypically in heterozygotes. The AB blood group system is the classical example.

Incomplete dominance occurs when the heterozygous phenotype is distinct from and often intermediate to the phenotypes of the homozygous phenotypes.

## Inheritance Patterns

### 1. Dominant-Recessive Inheritance

In dominantly inherited diseases, only one faulty gene is enough for manifestation of the disease. Examples include familial hypercholesterolemia and Huntington disease.

In recessive diseases, the faulty gene product is compensated by the normal variant. In this case, two mutated homologous genes are required for manifestation of the disease. Examples include cystic fibrosis and albinism.

Codominant inheritance is a variation of dominant-recessive inheritance. In codominant inheritance, two different alleles of a gene can be expressed, and each version makes a slightly different protein.

Blood type AB is inherited in a codominant pattern. If a person has one A blood group gene and one O blood group gene, the blood group will be A. If one A and one B are present, the blood group will be AB.

### 2. Polygenic Inheritance

In many cases, a trait is determined by more than one allele pair. Human examples include height, skin color, eye color, weight, diabetes mellitus, high blood pressure, asthma, allergy, and atherosclerosis.

### 3. Genetic Pleiotropy

An allele pair may be responsible for more than one trait. The gene product can participate in several metabolic pathways that affect different organs or tissues. A classic example is phenylketonuria (PKU), which can cause mental retardation and reduced hair and skin pigmentation.

### 4. Sex-Linked Inheritance

Humans have 46 chromosomes, consisting of 22 pairs of autosomes and two sex chromosomes. Females have two X chromosomes, while males have one X and one Y chromosome.

Genes on the X or Y chromosome are called sex-linked. Since humans have many more genes on the X than the Y, there are more X-linked traits than Y-linked traits. The gender of the offspring is determined by the sperm.

**Cytogenetics** is a field of genetics dealing with species- or cell-specific chromosome numbers and structure. With cytogenetic methods, the X and Y chromosomes can be differentiated.

Both chromosomes contain homologous and non-homologous regions. The non-homologous regions contain genes which do not have pairs in the other chromosome.

In males, these genes are in a hemizygotic state. Females possessing one X-linked recessive mutation are considered carriers and generally do not manifest clinical symptoms. All males possessing an X-linked recessive mutation will be affected, since males have only a single X chromosome.

All offspring of a carrier female have a 50% chance of inheriting the mutation if the father does not carry the recessive allele.

## The Patterns of Genetic Inheritance

### 1. Mendelian

- Mitochondrial
